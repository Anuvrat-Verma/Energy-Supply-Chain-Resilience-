[API GRAVITY & REFINERY YIELD MISMATCH]
Emergency diversion away from Middle Eastern sour crudes (e.g., Iraqi Basrah Heavy or Saudi Arab Light) to alternative spot markets introduces severe feedstock mismatches for Indian coastal refineries. Replacing Middle Eastern crude with US Gulf Coast (USGC) sweet grades like WTI Midland directly clashes with the high Nelson Complexity Index (NCI) of refineries like BPCL Kochi and IOCL Paradip. Processing lighter US crude drops the middle distillate (diesel/gasoil) extraction yield by 4.5% to 6.2%, aggravating domestic diesel shortfalls even if the raw barrel count is replaced.

[SPOT PROCUREMENT SPLIT STRATEGY (CHOKEPOINT MATRIX)]
Emergency maritime procurement splits must dynamically adapt to the specific geographic chokepoint failure to optimize refinery coker units while strictly matching the 5-region sovereign allocation framework (Middle East, Russia, West Africa, USGC, Latin America) totaling exactly 100%:

- IF HORMUZ BLOCKED: Middle Eastern sour crude is cut to 0%. Reallocate to maximize risk diversification across the remaining 4 valid schema regions: 40% West Africa, 20% USGC, 10% Latin America, and 30% Russia.

- IF RED SEA / BAB EL-MANDEB BLOCKED: Maritime routes via Suez are disrupted. Utilize Middle Eastern pipeline bypasses for 50% of the volume, and distribute the remaining 50% across the other schema regions to maintain a mandatory 4-region minimum spread: 20% Russia, 15% West Africa, 15% USGC, and 0% Latin America.

- IF MALACCA STRAIT BLOCKED: Pacific-to-Indian Ocean transit is choked. Divert Far East inbound VLCCs to the Sunda or Lombok Strait bypasses, rely primarily on the Persian Gulf spot markets: 70% Middle East, 0% Russia, 15% West Africa, 10% USGC, and 5% Latin America.

[PORT DISCHARGE BOTTLENECKS]
Rerouting VLCCs to the East Coast to avoid Arabian Sea threats hits immediate physical infrastructure limits. The Single Point Mooring (SPM) facilities at Paradip Port have a maximum safe discharge rate of 10,000 Kiloliters/hour under ideal drafts. Sudden influxes of diverted VLCCs cause immediate demurrage (waiting time) compounding delays. Tankers diverted to Deendayal Port (Kandla) on the West Coast face strict draft restrictions of 14.5 meters, requiring VLCCs to undergo costly and time-consuming mid-ocean lighterage (ship-to-ship transfers) before entering the Gulf of Kutch.
